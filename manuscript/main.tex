\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Abstract.}
Business process simulation (BPS) is widely used to evaluate process redesigns before interventions are implemented in real organizations. Existing data-driven BPS methods can learn control-flow, resource, and timing parameters from event logs, but they often represent resources as centralized pools or parameterized execution units. This study develops and evaluates a resource-centric LLM-augmented multi-agent process simulation artifact. The artifact extracts agent profiles, feasible action spaces, service-time samples, inter-event waiting-time samples, and handover priors from an event log, then compares a centralized baseline, a log-derived resource-agent baseline, and a constrained LLM-agent proxy. The implementation also includes an optional API-backed real LLM condition behind the same action-masked interface, with JSON validation and guarded fallback. Using the AcademicCredentials log from the Chapela-Campa et al. BPS quality artifact, repeated reproducible experiments show that the agent-profile condition performs best on formal control-flow n-gram and absolute/case-arrival timing distances, while the LLM-agent proxy is close on control-flow metrics and performs best on workforce Earth Mover's Distance. The central baseline remains strongest on several temporal distribution measures. The results do not support a claim that LLM-agent simulation is uniformly more accurate than traditional BPS. Instead, they show that log-grounded LLM-style decision modules can be integrated into a process-mining-compatible simulator and can add interpretable reasoning and handover outputs, but their quality must be evaluated as a dimension-specific trade-off.
\end{abstract}
