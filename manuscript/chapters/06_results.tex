The lightweight results suggest that the LLM-agent proxy is competitive
but not uniformly superior. It has the lowest mean trace variant
distance and mean cycle-time relative error in this metric set. However,
the agent-profile condition has the best activity distribution and
resource distribution distances. The resource result is particularly
important because the LLM-agent proxy was designed to alter local
resource choices. Although the proxy improves substantially over an
earlier greedy version, it still does not match the direct statistical
resource profile.

These results support two preliminary observations. First, a constrained
LLM-style local decision module can be integrated without destroying
basic control-flow or cycle-time behavior. Second, statistical resource
profiles remain strong baselines for reproducing resource allocation.
The LLM-agent condition should therefore be evaluated as an
interpretable extension with trade-offs, not as a simple replacement for
conventional BPS components.

\subsection{Formal Chapela-Campa Distance
Metrics}\label{formal-chapela-campa-distance-metrics}

picture. Table~\ref{tab:formal-metrics} reports selected Chapela-Campa
distance measures over ten generated logs per condition.

Figure~\ref{fig:formal-tradeoffs} visualizes the formal metric table on a common relative scale,
making clear that the experiment produces a trade-off profile rather
than a single winning policy. Each bar reports the mean distance divided
by the best mean for that metric, so the best condition for a metric has
value 1.0 and larger values indicate greater distance from the held-out
log.

The formal metrics revise the stronger impression given by the
lightweight table. The agent-profile condition is best on bigram and
trigram distance, indicating that it reproduces local control-flow
patterns slightly better than the other two policies. It is also best on
absolute event timing and case-arrival distance. The LLM-agent proxy is
close to the agent-profile baseline on n-gram metrics, but it does not
improve absolute timing or case arrivals. Its clearest advantage is
workforce EMD, where it achieves the best mean distance. The central
baseline performs best on circadian event distance, relative event-time
distance, and cycle-time Wasserstein distance.

The main empirical conclusion is therefore not that LLM-agent simulation
improves BPS quality overall. A more accurate conclusion is that the
LLM-agent proxy changes the quality profile of the simulator. It
preserves competitive control-flow behavior, improves workforce-distance
performance in this experiment, and provides additional reasoning and
handover traces, but it can underperform simpler baselines on temporal
distribution metrics. This is a useful result because it identifies
which behavioral dimensions require stronger modeling before a full
LLM-agent simulator can be claimed to improve process simulation
accuracy.

\subsection{Robustness Check on AgentSimulator
LoanApp}\label{robustness-check-on-agentsimulator-loanapp}

The second dataset provides a useful robustness check because it has a
clearer resource-role structure than AcademicCredentials. Under the
lightweight metrics, LoanApp is easier to reproduce overall: the
resource distribution distances are around 0.04--0.05 rather than above
0.60. However, the result does not make the LLM-agent proxy a universal
winner. The central baseline has the lowest trace, activity, and
resource distribution distance. The LLM-agent proxy is better than the
central baseline on mean cycle-time relative error and better than the
agent-profile policy on resource distribution distance, but not best
overall.

The central baseline remains strongest on control-flow n-grams, while
the LLM-agent proxy is strongest on workforce EMD and nearly tied with

LoanApp high-load what-if scenario is more favorable to the LLM-agent
proxy: it has the lowest mean cycle time, lowest p90 cycle time, and
highest throughput. Yet under the combined reduced-capacity and high-load
stress test, the proxy performs worse temporally. AgentSimulator LoanApp
therefore improves the empirical breadth of the study and shows where
the proxy can be advantageous, but it also reinforces that LLM-agent BPS
must be evaluated as a scenario-dependent trade-off.

\subsection{What-If Stress Test}\label{what-if-stress-test}

The previous results evaluate log reproduction. To examine whether the
artifact can also support what-if analysis, an additional stress test was
implemented. This experiment adds a simple resource-availability queue:
if a selected resource is still busy or unavailable, the event waits
until that resource becomes available. The intervention constrains the
top 20 high-frequency resources to 50\% capacity and increases workload
by compressing case arrivals with a 1.6x load multiplier. This combined
scenario is intentionally not a held-out-log reproduction task. It asks
how each policy reacts to a capacity and workload shock.

Table~\ref{tab:what-if} shows a different kind of result from the
reproduction metrics. The central baseline has the strongest temporal
response in this stress test: it produces the lowest mean and p90 cycle
time and the highest throughput. The LLM-agent proxy does not dominate
on these temporal measures, but it substantially reduces handovers per
case. The agent-profile policy relies most heavily on the constrained
high-frequency resource group and suffers the largest cycle-time
increase. This suggests that the value of an LLM-agent process simulator
should not be evaluated only by log similarity. It can also expose how
coordination style, resource concentration, and bottleneck behavior
change under interventions.

\subsection{Reasoning and Handover
Outputs}\label{reasoning-and-handover-outputs}

The LLM-agent proxy condition produces two additional artifacts beyond
the simulated event log. The reasoning log records the local decision
context and selected action for each generated event. The handover log
records transitions between resources when a case moves from one
resource to another. These logs do not directly improve the formal BPS
distance scores, but they change the observability of the simulator. A
conventional generated event log shows what happened; the reasoning and
handover logs provide a structured record of why a local assignment was
made and how work moved between resource agents.

This additional observability is important for future LLM-agent BPS. If
a real LLM is later used behind the same local policy interface,
reasoning and handover logs can support audits of invalid outputs,
fallback decisions, over-concentration on particular resources, and
inconsistencies between the natural-language rationale and the
executable event. In the current prototype, the logs demonstrate that
the proposed output structure \(O=(L', R', H')\) is technically feasible
and compatible with standard process-mining outputs.
